The t-test

The t-test is frequently used to compare the means of two samples or two measures on the same sample.

For example, we may wish to know whether the mean salary for men in some population is the same as the mean salary for women.  In this case we would have two independent groups of measures (mens' salaries and womens' salaries).

Or we might take two measures of scores on a maths quiz - one taken before a dose of fish oil is administered and one after.  In this case we have one group of subjects and two variables.

In each case we can use the t-test to determine if the null hypothesis that the means are equal is plausible.

There are more than one variant of the t-test to deal with these two different situations.

For the first we have the indpendent test.  This version of the test is sensitive to differences in the variance in scores between groups and before conducting the t-test you must establish whether or not these variances are more or less equal.

For the second we have the paired t-test.  This version is not sensitive differences in variances between the two measures.

R provides one function for both these variants.
